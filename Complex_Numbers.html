Complex Numbers for Electrical Engineering

Complex numbers are fundamental mathematical tools in electrical engineering, providing elegant solutions for analyzing AC circuits, signal processing, and control systems. This guide will take you from basic concepts to practical engineering applications.

1. Fundamentals of Complex Numbers

1.1 Definition and Basic Representation

A complex number \(z\) combines two components:

  • A real part (\(a\))
  • An imaginary part (\(b\))

Written in standard form as:

\[ z = a + bi \]

In electrical engineering, we use \(j\) instead of \(i\) to avoid confusion with current notation:

\[ z = a + jb \]

Where \(j\) is the imaginary unit defined by \(j^2 = -1\)

Example:

\(z = 3 + j4\) has real part \(3\) and imaginary part \(4\).

[Visualization: A simple diagram showing a complex number as a point in the complex plane with labeled real and imaginary components]

1.2 The Complex Plane

Every complex number corresponds to a unique point in the complex plane:

  • The horizontal axis represents the real component
  • The vertical axis represents the imaginary component

This geometric interpretation lets us visualize complex numbers as vectors from the origin.

[Visualization: The complex plane with labeled axes and several example points]

1.3 Key Forms of Complex Numbers

Rectangular form: \(z = a + jb\)

  • Most familiar representation
  • Directly shows real and imaginary components
  • Best for addition and subtraction
[Visualization: Rectangular form representation showing real and imaginary axes]

Converting between forms:

  • Rectangular to polar: \(r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\), \(\theta = \tan^{-1}(b/a)\)
  • Polar to rectangular: \(a = r\cos \theta\), \(b = r\sin \theta\)
  • Rectangular to exponential: \(z = a + jb = re^{j\theta}\) where \(r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\) and \(\theta = \tan^{-1}(b/a)\)

2. Operations with Complex Numbers

2.1 Addition and Subtraction

Add or subtract the real and imaginary parts separately:

\[ z_1 + z_2 = (a_1 + jb_1) + (a_2 + jb_2) = (a_1 + a_2) + j(b_1 + b_2) \]
\[ z_1 - z_2 = (a_1 + jb_1) - (a_2 + jb_2) = (a_1 - a_2) + j(b_1 - b_2) \]

Example:

\((3 + j4) + (2 - j5) = (3 + 2) + j(4 - 5) = 5 - j1\)

Geometrically, addition is similar to vector addition in the complex plane.

[Visualization: Vector addition of complex numbers in the complex plane]

2.2 Multiplication

Multiply using algebraic distribution and remember that \(j^2 = -1\):

\[ (a_1 + jb_1)(a_2 + jb_2) = a_1a_2 + ja_1b_2 + jb_1a_2 + j^2b_1b_2 \] \[ = (a_1a_2 - b_1b_2) + j(a_1b_2 + b_1a_2) \]

Example:

\((3 + j4)(2 - j5) = 3 \cdot 2 - 3 \cdot j5 + j4 \cdot 2 + j4 \cdot (-j5)\)

\(= 6 - j15 + j8 - j^2 \cdot 20 = 6 - j15 + j8 + 20 = 26 - j7\)

Multiplication in polar form:

In polar form, multiplication becomes much simpler:

\[ z_1 \cdot z_2 = r_1e^{j\theta_1} \cdot r_2e^{j\theta_2} = r_1r_2e^{j(\theta_1+\theta_2)} \]

This means:

  • Multiply the magnitudes
  • Add the angles

2.3 Complex Conjugate

The complex conjugate of \(z = a + jb\) is defined as:

\[ z^* = a - jb \]

Properties of the complex conjugate:

  • \(z \cdot z^* = |z|^2 = a^2 + b^2\)
  • \((z_1 + z_2)^* = z_1^* + z_2^*\)
  • \((z_1 \cdot z_2)^* = z_1^* \cdot z_2^*\)
[Visualization: Complex conjugate as reflection across the real axis]

2.4 Division

Division uses the complex conjugate technique:

\[ \frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{a_1 + jb_1}{a_2 + jb_2} = \frac{(a_1 + jb_1)(a_2 - jb_2)}{(a_2 + jb_2)(a_2 - jb_2)} = \frac{(a_1a_2 + b_1b_2) + j(b_1a_2 - a_1b_2)}{a_2^2 + b_2^2} \]

Example:

\[\frac{3 + j4}{2 + j5} = \frac{(3 + j4)(2 - j5)}{(2 + j5)(2 - j5)} = \frac{26 - j7}{4 + 25} = \frac{26 - j7}{29} = \frac{26}{29} - j\frac{7}{29}\]

Division in polar form:

In polar form, division is:

\[ \frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{r_1e^{j\theta_1}}{r_2e^{j\theta_2}} = \frac{r_1}{r_2}e^{j(\theta_1-\theta_2)} \]

This means:

  • Divide the magnitudes
  • Subtract the angles

2.5 Powers and Roots

De Moivre's formula allows us to compute powers of complex numbers:

\[ (re^{j\theta})^n = r^ne^{jn\theta} = r^n(\cos(n\theta) + j\sin(n\theta)) \]

For roots:

\[ z^{1/n} = r^{1/n}e^{j(\theta+2\pi k)/n} \]

where \(k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n-1\), giving \(n\) distinct roots.

3. Applications in Electrical Engineering

3.1 Impedance and Admittance

Complex impedance combines resistance and reactance:

\[ Z = R + jX \]

Where:

  • \(R\) is resistance (real part, measured in ohms)
  • \(X\) is reactance (imaginary part, measured in ohms)
    • For inductors: \(X_L = \omega L\) (positive)
    • For capacitors: \(X_C = -\frac{1}{\omega C}\) (negative)

The complex admittance is the reciprocal of impedance:

\[ Y = \frac{1}{Z} = G + jB \]

Where:

  • \(G\) is conductance (real part, measured in siemens)
  • \(B\) is susceptance (imaginary part, measured in siemens)

Example:

A circuit with a resistor (\(R = 100\Omega\)) in series with an inductor (\(L = 50mH\)) at a frequency of \(\omega = 1000\) rad/s has an impedance of:

\(Z = 100 + j(1000 \times 0.05) = 100 + j50\Omega\)

The magnitude of this impedance is:

\(|Z| = \sqrt{100^2 + 50^2} = \sqrt{12500} = 111.8\Omega\)

The phase angle is:

\(\theta = \tan^{-1}(50/100) = 26.6°\)

[Visualization: Impedance triangle showing relationship between R, X, and |Z|]

3.2 Phasors and AC Analysis

A sinusoidal signal \(v(t) = V_m\cos(\omega t + \phi)\) can be represented by the complex phasor:

\[ V = V_m e^{j\phi} \]

This transforms time-domain differential equations into algebraic equations in the frequency domain:

\[ v(t) = V_m\cos(\omega t + \phi) \Rightarrow V = V_m\angle\phi \]

Engineering Insight:

Phasors only work for sinusoidal signals of the same frequency. The real, time-domain signal is understood to be the real part of the complex expression \(Ve^{j\omega t}\).

Ohm's Law in AC Circuits:

For AC circuit analysis:

\[ V = ZI \]

Where \(V\) and \(I\) are phasors representing voltage and current, and \(Z\) is the complex impedance.

This means:

  • Magnitudes are related by \(|V| = |Z| \cdot |I|\)
  • Phase angles are related by \(\phi_V = \phi_Z + \phi_I\)
[Visualization: Phasor diagram showing relationship between current and voltage phasors]

3.3 Frequency Response and Transfer Functions

Transfer functions in control systems and signal processing are often expressed as functions of the complex variable \(s = \sigma + j\omega\) (in the Laplace domain) or \(z = re^{j\theta}\) (in the Z-domain for discrete systems).

Example: RC Low-Pass Filter

A simple RC low-pass filter has the transfer function:

\[ H(j\omega) = \frac{1}{1 + j\omega RC} \]

The magnitude response is:

\[ |H(j\omega)| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (\omega RC)^2}} \]

And the phase response is:

\[ \angle H(j\omega) = -\tan^{-1}(\omega RC) \]
[Visualization: Bode plot showing magnitude and phase response of an RC filter]

3.4 Power Calculations

The apparent power \(S\) in an AC circuit is a complex number:

\[ S = P + jQ \]

Where:

  • \(P\) is the real power (measured in watts) - power that performs work
  • \(Q\) is the reactive power (measured in volt-amperes reactive) - power that is stored and released in reactive components

For a load with voltage \(V\) and current \(I\):

\[ S = VI^* \]

Power Factor:

The power factor is given by:

\[ PF = \cos\theta = \frac{P}{|S|} \]

Where \(\theta\) is the phase angle between voltage and current.

  • PF = 1: Purely resistive load (ideal)
  • PF = 0: Purely reactive load (no real power consumed)
[Visualization: Power triangle showing relationship between P, Q, and |S|]

Example:

If a load draws a current of \(5\angle{-30°}\) A with an applied voltage of \(120\angle{0°}\) V, the complex power is:

\(S = VI^* = 120\angle{0°} \times 5\angle{30°} = 600\angle{30°} = 519.6 + j300\) VA

The power factor is:

\(PF = \cos(30°) = 0.866\)

Conclusion

Complex numbers are an essential mathematical tool in electrical engineering, providing elegant solutions to problems involving oscillations, waves, and AC circuits. Understanding complex numbers and their operations is fundamental for advanced concepts in signal processing, control systems, and electromagnetic theory.

Key Takeaways:

  • Complex numbers allow us to represent magnitude and phase information simultaneously
  • The exponential form \(re^{j\theta}\) greatly simplifies AC circuit analysis
  • Impedance \(Z = R + jX\) combines resistance and reactance into a single quantity
  • Phasors transform differential equations into algebraic equations for sinusoidal steady state
